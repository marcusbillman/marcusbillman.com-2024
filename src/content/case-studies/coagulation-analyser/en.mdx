In 2022 and 2023, I was involved with the research and development of a new analysis device for coagulation testing. Such equipment is used in hospital labs to analyse patient blood samples, and this new device brought a feature set and user experience not seen before in its category.

My work began as a 14-week internship and was later extended with a 7-month project employment. During the project, I led the UX/UI process, going from a feature spec to a high-fidelity interactive UI prototype and a detailed design system of components and guidelines.

# What is coagulation testing?

When you get a cut, your blood forms a clot to stop the bleeding—a process called _coagulation_. At a hospital, they can measure how well your body does this through _coagulation testing_.

A sample of your blood is mixed with a _reagent_ that triggers clotting. A coagulation analyser measures how long this takes, often by sensing changes in light passing through the sample. Too slow clotting means higher bleeding risk, while too fast clotting means higher risk of dangerous blood clots.

There are different kinds of tests used to draw different conclusions. For example, a D-dimer test can help determine whether you have a serious blood clot, and a PT test is used to monitor blood-thinning medications like warfarin.

# The mission

The device in question is a semi-automatic coagulation analyser. It sits on a desk and is operated manually, as opposed to large, automatic analysers that process samples robotically.

Unfortunately, semi-automatic analysers are not where the innovation happens. Most of them have limited functionality, outdated user interfaces, and poor build quality. This new analyser aims to change that, bringing features and performance closer to the automatic realm, while being easier to use and feeling premium.

My mission was to design a UI from the ground up that captures this vision. Both novice and seasoned lab staff should be able to run analyses, follow them in real-time, check results, view instructions, calibrate the device, track reagent stock, and more.

# Competitive analysis

The project began with learning how coagulation testing is performed on currently available analysers. This included hands-on demonstrations in the company's private labs, as well as walkthroughs of analysers from different manufacturers. This gave me a feel for the workflows, features and UI patterns.

I noticed these core pain points in the semi-automatic analysers:

- **There are many manual steps,** such as precise pipetting, memorising procedures and handling patient samples. Each step increases the risk for error.

- **UIs are not very intuitive,** with poorly labelled buttons and lack of guidance to the next step. This increases mental load and makes the analyser harder to learn.

- **Functionality is lacking,** such as reaction curves and error detection during analysis.

Based on those, I established three design principles:

- **Clarity and safety:** The analyser should clearly communicate status and help fix issues. Users should be guided before, during and after analysis to prevent mistakes.

- **Simplicity and modern patterns:** The UI should take cues from smartphones and other devices to make navigation intuitive. The user shouldn't be overwhelmed with information.

- **Fresh look and feel:** A sense of quality and innovation should be reflected through a clean, contrast-rich and trustworthy design language based on the company's brand identity.

Based on the briefing and feature specification, I created an information architecture and wireframes for core screens. I then turned these into low-fidelity mockups, regularly asking for feedback from lab technicians at the company.

After exploring several layouts, I settled on a left main navigation to efficiently use the landscape screen. Originally I placed the status bar at the bottom, but through testing found it better suited at the top. This way, it's out of the way of the four sample slots below the screen.

Compared to competing analysers, I wanted to reduce the amount of rarely used buttons cluttering the navigation, and create distinct flows and screens for routine tasks, such as calibration and inserting patient samples.

I designed numerous core workflows and interaction patterns from the ground up, including parallel analyses, expandable live sensor graphs, sample queue management, notification systems, and consumable inventory tracking.

# User tests

Throughout the project, I planned and conducted multiple rounds of observational user tests with lab technicians. I evaluated different flows and scenarios by assigning realistic laboratory tasks and observing natural behaviour. For realism and context, the interactive prototypes ran on a touch screen mounted on a device prototype, accompanied by real lab equipment such as pipettes and test tubes.

From the first two rounds, I collected over 100 points of feedback, ranked them by impact, and used them to iterate on the design.

# The result

The result is a well-structured, tangible UI that supports lab technicians through a plethora of routine tasks. The visuals are simple, crisp, and communicate clearly with consistent use of colour. It's a precise blend of data, pedagogy, and aesthetics.

<GalleryBlock
  items={[

Interactive charts and clear graphics take advantage of the large touch screen for quick scannability in the lab. During analysis, on-screen elements are visually connected to the physical sample slots for an intuitive workflow. Step-by-step instructions, visual cues, and warnings help prevent mistakes, and the top-left status indicator always shows what's happening.

# Conclusion

While the team and decision makers were satisfied with the UI, the strongest validation came from lab technicians I tested with. Even those with hospital lab backgrounds consistently praised the interface as being way ahead of any other semi-automatic coagulation analyser they'd used.

This was the largest design project I'd worked on. I collaborated with software engineers and laboratory staff, as well as external industrial designers, electronics engineers and branding designers. In addition to the core UX/UI work, I practised skills such as

- communicating design decisions with engineers and other stakeholders
- planning and conducting user tests and aggregating data
- creating and documenting design systems in Figma
- planning and presenting my design process.

This project was incredibly rewarding. I especially loved working with inspiring colleagues while contributing meaningfully to a product that could save lives. This device has real positive potential for healthcare, particularly in underserved regions, and I'm excited to see how it develops further.
